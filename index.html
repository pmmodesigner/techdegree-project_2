Philosophy

Three design criteria…

Still a young designer, I've got a lifetime of learning in the craft of visual communication. However, I've discovered that I arrive to my work with a set of design criteria to yield powerful design work:

I firmly believe that compelling visual communication occurs when it is clear, accessible, and impressive. Clear to immediately relay its message; so it should be simple. Accessible to make it relatable and understood; so it should be human. And impressive to engender a spirit of wonderment; so it should be artful.

These three criteria have guided me to create design work that I believe communicates effectively and even viscerally.

Design is simple.

Every once in a while, I get a feeling that everything has become too much: a constant hum of a furnace, a spew of photos and drawings on a refrigerator, or a super sweet latte—not even potable.

I surmise that supersaturation isn't desired.

I am convinced that a plethora of words and objects in a design layout wouldn't allow me the ability to effortlessly gather the message being presented to me.

Simplicity isn't a means of getting rid of something; it is a means of leaving the perfect amount of something—all to avoid the too much and to elevate the remaining into elegance.

Design is human.

Humans have developed language­—a way to effectively communicate.

However, I propose that symbols have a greater emotional pull than language: symbols provide illustrations of human processes. For example, they give insight as to how a tribe hunted and survived via wall paintings, they demand auto drivers to stop via red octagons, and they even unify whole belief systems in a few straightforward shapes via pictograms.

These ideas are easily conveyed without the mechanical syntax and lengthiness of language. Being mindful of regional cultural ideas, they allow elaborate concepts to become highly accesible to all persons.

Design is artful.

I see art as being a creative and expressive experience. An experience derived from an artist's intention and a viewer's interpretation. And due to this dichotomy of artist intention versus viewer interpretation, it is a way to connect at a profound level by holding a dialogue with a silent, unknown other.

It is at this dialogue where truths may be murmured; where emotions may be stirred; where divides may be bridged; and where minds may be marveled. Pieces of artwork comprise a network of all of the creations and expressions made by mankind. They become the cultural identity of our human race.

This makes it imperative that all of my works should be made with craft and consideration for my viewers' interpretation.

I started my design career clear back in middle school. For some reason, I always found myself attracted to the package design of the groceries that my papá and mamá brought home from the market.

Realizing that my high school offered a computer arts class, I signed up at once.

Delving into photo manipulation, typography, pre-press, and some identity work, I was fortunate enough to start competing with other high schools state-wide and nationally. In 2009, I was honored with a second place award in Advertising Design at the National Leadership and Skills Conference in Kansas City, MO.

I proceeded to college at Idaho State University with a major in mass communication with an emphasis in graphic design.

In college, I took up a job designing posters for student body functions. And after college, I was offered a position at a local print shop, solidifying my print and pre-press skills. Later, I worked for a sign studio in Sun Valley, Idaho—taking my print skills into the third dimension. Currently, I'm employed by a nationally ranked newspaper as a layout editor.

And now I'm ready to propell myself from a print space to a digital one, visually communicating for future clients with my newly acquired coding skills on websites and applications.
